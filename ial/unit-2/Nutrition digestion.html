Enzymes as Biological Catalysts

2.10-2.11

üí° What are enzymes? Think of enzymes like helpful machines in a factory. They speed up chemical reactions without getting used up!

Enzyme

A biological catalyst - a protein that speeds up chemical reactions in living organisms without being used up.

Active Site

A special pocket on the enzyme where the substrate fits perfectly - like a lock and key!

Key Points to Remember:
‚úì Enzymes are proteins
‚úì They speed up reactions
‚úì They are NOT used up (can be reused)
‚úì Each enzyme only works on one type of substrate (specific)

What is the name for the special part of an enzyme where the substrate attaches?

The reaction site
The active site
The enzyme pocket
The binding zone

Enzymes are biological catalysts that speed up metabolic reactions without being changed or used up.

Lock and Key Model: The substrate fits precisely into the enzyme's active site, forming an enzyme-substrate complex. The reaction occurs, products are released, and the enzyme is free to work again.
‚ö†Ô∏è Exam Tip

Always describe enzymes as "biological catalysts" not just "catalysts" - this shows you understand they are found in living organisms.

Induced Fit Model

The active site changes shape slightly when the substrate binds, moulding around it for a closer fit and helping to catalyse the reaction.

How enzymes lower activation energy:
‚Ä¢ Bringing substrates together correctly
‚Ä¢ Putting strain on bonds within the substrate
‚Ä¢ Providing a microenvironment with different conditions

  • ‚Ä¢ Induced fit explains how binding strains substrate bonds (1)
  • ‚Ä¢ Shape change helps release products (1)
  • ‚Ä¢ Induced fit better accounts for competitive inhibitor binding (1)

Effect of Temperature on Enzymes

2.11-2.12

üí° Think about cooking an egg: When you heat an egg, the proteins change shape. This is similar to what happens to enzymes if they get too hot!

What happens at different temperatures:

ü•∂ Too cold: Molecules move slowly ‚Üí fewer collisions ‚Üí slow reaction

‚úÖ Optimum (37-40¬∞C): Perfect balance ‚Üí fastest reaction

üî• Too hot: Enzyme changes shape (DENATURED) ‚Üí stops working

Denaturation

When an enzyme's shape is permanently changed (usually by heat or wrong pH), so the substrate can no longer fit in the active site.

Increasing temp (up to optimum):
‚Ä¢ More kinetic energy
‚Ä¢ More collisions
‚Ä¢ Faster reaction
Beyond optimum:
‚Ä¢ Hydrogen bonds break
‚Ä¢ Active site changes shape
‚Ä¢ Enzyme denatured (permanent)
‚ö†Ô∏è Exam Tip

Never say the enzyme is "killed" - enzymes are not alive! Say the enzyme is denatured and the active site changes shape.

Why does enzyme activity decrease above the optimum temperature?

The enzyme is killed by the heat
The substrate moves too fast
The active site changes shape so substrate can't fit
The enzyme runs out of energy
Q‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄ coefficient: For every 10¬∞C rise, the rate approximately doubles (up to optimum). This is due to increased kinetic energy and more successful collisions.
