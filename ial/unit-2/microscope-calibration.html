Understanding the Equipment

Before we begin calculations, you need to understand the two key pieces of equipment used in calibration.

Eyepiece Graticule

A glass disc with an arbitrary scale

Stage Micrometer

A slide with a precise, known scale

Key difference: The stage micrometer has real measurements (micrometers), while the eyepiece graticule has arbitrary divisions with no inherent value ‚Äî that's why we need to calibrate it!

Calibration value = Stage micrometer reading (¬µm)/Number of graticule divisions

Why Recalibration Matters

This is a crucial concept that examiners love to test. Understanding why is more important than just knowing the rule.

The Rule: You MUST recalibrate the eyepiece graticule every time you change the objective lens (magnification).
Consider: The eyepiece graticule is inside the eyepiece ‚Äî does it change when you switch objectives? What about the image of the specimen/stage micrometer?
Model Answer (3 marks)
  • ‚úì The eyepiece graticule remains the same size (it's fixed inside the eyepiece)
  • ‚úì When magnification increases, the image of the specimen/stage micrometer appears larger
  • ‚úì Therefore, fewer graticule divisions will cover the same actual distance, changing the calibration value

Common Misconceptions

"The graticule divisions get bigger at higher magnification"
‚úì Actually: The graticule never changes size ‚Äî it's a physical piece of glass in the eyepiece. Only the image of the specimen changes size.
"You only need to calibrate once per microscope"
‚úì Actually: You need to calibrate for each magnification you use. The calibration value will be different at √ó100 vs √ó400.
